What is Scrum

From the Scrum Guide

Scrum is a framwork for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products.
This is quoted from the Scrum Guide. And this is very true. This is about how to develop, deliver and maintain products.

Cynefin Model

This is the cynefin model to aid decision-making.
  • Obvious (or simple): This is obvious to the person in that domain. If the problem is obvious, You've got to get a sense of what the problem is- categorize the problem, and respond. This means that there are rules in place/best practices, the problem is very clear and the situation is stable. There's likely only one right answer (known knowns).
  • Complicated: This requires analysis. So first, you get a sense of what the problem is, analyze the problem, and respond. There's probably a range of right answers (known unknowns)
  • Complex: This can't be analyzed, and requires some kind of probing (experimenting), then sensing- which is analyzing the data, and then responding. (unknown unknowns)
  • Chaos is just bad. (Run) This is when action is required up front to establish some order, then sensing, and then responding. (Stop the bleeding)
    • Scrum lives between the Complicated domain and the complex one, to be able to take the complex problems, break them down into complicated ones. Where each sprint is kind of an experiment of sorts.

Characteristics of Scrum

  • Lightweight, simple to understand, difficult to master
  • Each component serve a specific purpose
  • Empirical process: Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation
Scrum is lightweight, simple to understand, but difficult to master. Each component (i.e. roles and ceremonies) serves a purpose, and specifc responsibilities. There are 3 pillars to Scrum:

*click* Transparency: Aspects of the process must be visible to those responsible for the outcome. These aspects should be defined by a common standard so everyone shares a common understanding of what is being seen. This takes place in a non-judgemental environment. It's everyone's problem, therefore everyone's responsible for the solution.

*click* Inspection: Members should be frequently inspecting the scrum artifacts and progress toward a sprint goal. But shouldn't be so frequent that it's an impediment.

*click* Adaptation: If during the inspection process, we start to see deviations, we need to address the issue, and course correct.

Characteristics of Scrum (cont)

  • Individuals & Interactions over Process & Tools
  • Working Software over Comprehensive Documentation
  • Customer Collaboration over Contract Negotiation
  • Responding to Change over Following a plan

These come from the manifesto. And are built on that agile is about people

Individuals and Interactions over Process and Tools. Agile is about people. Don't get caught up in "Don't do the smart thing because it's not in our process" or "Let's do the dumb thing because it's our process"

Working software over comprehensive documentation. no need to over document code, or proceedures, getting the software to work and be consumable is the priority

Customer Collaboration over Contract Negotiation. This allows us to give what the customers need- not necessarily what they want.

Responding to change over following a plan. We should be responsive and flexible to circumstances, and not dig our heals into any specific plan

This does not mean just throw away the left. But enough of what's on the right *click*, to make the left *click* awesome.

Important parts of Scrum

Retrospective is the most important part of Scrum. Backlog refinement is the second most important.

Retrospective is the most important part of Scrum. This is the part that takes inspection to adaptation. You have to change 1, and only 1 thing to improve. These can be impactful, so avoiding blaming is important. In fact, one of the things that Bob tried is banning the word "because" or "because of" in his retros. Then, backlog refinement is the second most important thing. Again, this an area that takes inspection to adaptation. We don't want to go down one path and develop something wasteful/pointless. We have to be able to adapt our product backlog too.

Scrum Team

  • Product Owner : Responsible for the product backlog
  • Development Team : Responsible for the increment (sprint deliverable)
  • Scrum Master : Responsible for promoting and supporting Scrum. Servant-leader
  • *click* The product owner is responsible for maximizing the value of the product backlog. This includes making it clear, and visible, and making sure that the backlog is in priority order.
  • *click* The Development team is responsible for delivering a releasable increment of done at the end of each sprint. They are self organizing, and cross functional. There's no sub teams nor titles.
  • *click* The Scrum Master helps promote scrum. They are a servant-leader for the Scrum Team and maximizes the value produced by the team. They are there to serve the team, and aspire to lead them to success. They are often the facilitator between business needs, and development needs.

Scrum Ceremonies

  • The Sprint : Time-box of the development of a "done", potentially releaseable product Increment
  • Sprint Planning : Planning of what work will be performed in the Sprint
  • Daily Scrum : Time-box to forecast the the next 24-hours of work of the Dev Team
  • Sprint Review : Review the last Increment and adapt the P.B.
  • Scrum Retrospective : Vent session : Inspection of the last sprint and create plan for improvements

*click* The sprint is a time frame of when a product Increment is created. This product increment is a useable, and possibly releasable piece of functionality. The sprint contains the planning, daily scrums, the development work, the sprint review, and the retro.

*click* The Sprint Planning answers the questions of what can be delivered in the Increment rsulting from the upcoming Sprint? And how will the work needed to deliver the Increment be acheived

*click* The Daily Scrum is a short 15 minute daily meeting in the DEV team to plan work for the next 24 hours. This is to optimize the performance. This is a big proponent for the "Adaptation" pillar of the Empirical process.

*click* The Sprint Review is to Review the last increment developed during that sprint. This gives a chance to adapt the product backlog if necessary. This meeting is not a status meeting. This is a presentation to elicit feedback- and Bob says that it should be a positive feedback, not a time to be critical or add a wish list.

*click* The Retro is an opportunity for the Scrum Team to inspect itself and create a plan for improvements. This inspection is in regards to people, relationships, process, and tools. Bob mentioned you should limit to 1 action item.

Take Aways

Reasons Sprint Teams fail

  • Routinely carrying over work from one sprint to the next
  • They can't work quality into a Sprint
  • Lousy Product Backlog

50% of work can be a waste

,"Bad Stuff", "Good Stuff", "Great Stuff" My first dataset, 50, 30, 20

In our class, it was said that on average, the last (right) half of the product backlog, is filled with work isn't used, or doesn't affect the users. Or in some fashion, this last (right) half is a waste of time.

That might not be the case for us, but does give an area to inspect for waste. That doesn't mean the "Bad stuff" isn't stuff we shouldn't implement, it's just saying that they might be time wasters, or not as profitable as the "Great/Good" stuff.

(Show drawing) So let's say we have project a, and project b, both with dollar values upon completing the product backlog. If we split each product in half, Project B's first "Great and good " stuff might be worth more than project a's bad stuff. (Or "not good enough stuff"). So it's more beneficial to have Each projects' first half done and released before their second halves.

Tuckman Model

There was an interesting model shown called the Tuckman model. These are the phases a team would go through. First is the Forming stage. This is where it's just introductory.

The next is storming where they are figuring each other's boundaries, and maybe being slightly combative, but because they are pushing each other, and being a little adventurous with presenting their thoughts, they are more productive. Then after sometime they start to mesh together and come to solutions a bit faster. That's the norming phase.

After they start to build their solution-creating skills together, they start to speak the same language and work seemlessly. This the performing team. This is estimated to be 50% more productivite than the initial forming team.

If we are every quarter or every other sprint shuffling around we might not be through that stroming phase long enough to learn how to be productive for long. Making us lose out on getting stuff done faster, and potential money.

Project estimates

  • Fixed Scope = Flexible time
  • Flexible Scope = Fixed time
  • Fixed Scope + Fixed Time = Pad the release date by A LOT.
When it comes to estimating projects, this is a scrum team effort. And there are some constraints to understand.

*click* If the scope is fixed, then the release date must be flexible.

*click* If the release date is fixed, then the scope must be flexible.

*click* If the scope and release date must be fixed, then that release date must be padded by a lot.

Say no

Be able to say "No, why offer alternatives/solutions"

All members of the scrum team need to have the ability to say no. But just saying no isn't very well received / might not make a difference. So saying no in this no, why, alternatives structure actually can be very productive.

Empowerment Bill of Rights

  1. Build the person up
  2. Clearly frame the Empowerment
  3. Give contraints
  4. Give advice
  5. Test it
This is something we should implement to our development team. Give them the path to be successful by giving the courage and tools to be successful. And set up a route to fail, I mean, learn fast.
